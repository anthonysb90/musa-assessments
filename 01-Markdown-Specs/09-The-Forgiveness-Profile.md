## What it measures

Forgiveness is one of the hardest and most freeing things we do. This reflective profile brings to mind someone who hurt you, then looks at the many reasons that draw a person to forgive, from your own peace, to care for the other, to your faith. Over 30 short statements, it shows which motivations are strongest in you right now. Built on the research-based Enright Forgiveness Motivation Inventory.

It looks at:

- 10 motivations, from Self-Healing to Consistency with Your Beliefs
- Your overall motivation to forgive
- Your unique profile: which reasons pull hardest on your heart

**Domains / scales (10):** Protection for Others Inside the Family, Forgiveness as Good in Itself, Consistency with Your Beliefs, Healing for the Other, Self-Healing, Community Harmony, Self-Improvement, Protection for Others Outside the Family, Improved Relationship, Improvement for the Other.

## Part 1 — The Reflection Scale (comes before the 30 items)

The 30 motivation items all begin "I forgive because…", so they only make sense once the person has a specific offense in mind and has said where they stand with forgiving it. This reflection is taken **first**. (Instrument pages 3-4.)

> _Prompt shown:_ We are sometimes unfairly hurt by other people, whether in family, friendship, school, work, or other situations. Bring to mind the most severe experience of someone being very unfair to you. For a few moments, picture what happened. Try to see the person, and try to recall what it was like. You'll answer the rest of this reflection with that one experience in mind.

**Recall questions (context — not scored, stored with the result):**

1. How deeply were you hurt when it happened? No hurt / A little hurt / Some hurt / Much hurt / A great deal of hurt (1–5)
2. Who hurt you? Child, Spouse, Relative, Parent, Friend, Employer, Other (with a write-in for Other)
3. Is that person living? Yes / No
4. How long ago was the offense? A number plus a unit (days, weeks, months, years)
5. Briefly, what happened? (free text, optional, private)

**Forgiveness status:**

6. Have you forgiven the person mentioned above? Yes / No
7. To what degree have you forgiven them? 1=Not at all / 3=In progress / 5=Complete forgiveness (1–5 continuum)

**Definition of forgiveness — comprehension check (this is scored):**

The person picks the ONE statement they think best defines what it means to forgive. Only two of the eight describe genuine forgiveness. This tells you whether the rest of their answers rest on an accurate understanding of forgiveness or a common look-alike (reconciliation, forgetting, simply moving on, easing one's own anger). The instrument's own scoring notes (pages 12-14) are the source for each verdict.

| # | Definition statement | Verdict | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | When people forgive, they try to rise above their angry feelings by thinking positive thoughts about issues that do not necessarily relate to the injustice. | Misconception | Forgiveness is a moral response toward a person, not a situation. Only easing your own anger keeps the focus on yourself, not on the one who acted unjustly. Forgiveness offers mercy to that person. |
| 2 | When people forgive, they come together with the one who offended them and so they have mutual trust. | Misconception | This describes reconciliation, not forgiveness. Reconciliation is two people coming back together in mutual trust. A person can forgive and still not reconcile. |
| 3 | When people forgive, they stop seeking a fair solution to the problem between themselves and the people who behaved badly. | Misconception | Forgiveness is about mercy, not justice. You can forgive and still seek a fair outcome. Forgiveness and justice can happen together. |
| 4 | When people forgive, they try, as best they can today, to be good to the one who offended them. | Near-accurate | This is close to a complete definition. What it is missing is the idea of reducing resentment toward the person. |
| 5 | When people forgive, they can let go of what happened to them and so it no longer bothers those who forgive. | Misconception | A person can move past the incident yet still quietly dismiss the offender as less than human. Forgiveness includes a change of heart toward the person, not only relief from the memory. |
| 6 | When people forgive, they try to reduce resentment and offer love to the other for the other's sake. | Accurate | This is an accurate definition. It reduces the negative feelings toward the offender and adds the positive, especially love offered for the other person's sake. |
| 7 | When people forgive, the key issue is that they move on from the situation by putting it behind them. | Misconception | This can be a way of diverting attention away from what happened and from the offender. To forgive is to have mercy on the person, not to move past the event. |
| 8 | When people forgive, the key issue is that they try to forget the injustice that happened to them. | Misconception | When people forgive, they stay aware that the injustice was real and still choose to offer mercy to the offender. Forgiveness is not forgetting. |

**How the definition is scored / reported:**

- **Accurate -> "A clear understanding of forgiveness"**: You chose a definition that describes genuine forgiveness: reducing resentment and offering goodwill to the one who hurt you, for their sake. That is a strong foundation. It means the reflection you just did rests on the real thing, not a look-alike.
- **Near-accurate -> "A close understanding of forgiveness"**: You chose a definition that is close to the real thing: choosing to be good to the one who hurt you. The one piece it leaves out is the inner work of letting resentment go. Genuine forgiveness is both, softening toward the person and offering goodwill.
- **Misconception -> "A common misunderstanding of forgiveness"**: The definition you chose is one many people hold, but it describes something other than forgiveness, often reconciliation, forgetting, simply moving on, or easing your own anger. Genuine forgiveness means reducing resentment toward the person and offering them goodwill for their sake, while still being honest that the wrong was real. Knowing that changes how the rest of this reflection reads.

Only statements 4 (near-accurate) and 6 (accurate) reflect genuine forgiveness. Choosing 6 = a clear understanding; 4 = close; any other = a common misunderstanding. The report shows the person their result gently; the exact verdict and explanation are recorded for the admin.

## How it is scored

**Type:** Subscale sums (10 motivation subscales).

1. Answers use a 6-point scale (1 Strongly Disagree → 6 Strongly Agree). There is no neutral midpoint, by design.
2. The 30 items map to **10 motivation subscales, 3 items each**. Items are mixed in order, not grouped.
3. Each subscale score is the **sum** of its 3 items → range **3–18**.
4. Subscale bands:

| Band | Score |
|---|---|
| Strong | ≥ 15 (of 18) |
| Moderate | ≥ 9 (of 18) |
| Quiet | ≥ 0 (of 18) |

5. The **total** is the sum of all 30 items → range **30–180**. Total bands: ≥135 'strong, broad heart to forgive', ≥75 'genuine and growing', below 75 'forgiveness may feel far off right now, and that's okay'.
6. Subscales are ranked so the taker sees which reasons pull hardest.

The 10 subscales: Self-Healing, Self-Improvement, Healing for the Other, Improvement for the Other, Improved Relationship, Protection for Others Outside the Family, Protection for Others Inside the Family, Forgiveness as Good in Itself, Community Harmony, Consistency with Your Beliefs.

### Worked example

If the three 'Self-Healing' items are answered 6, 5, 6 → subscale = 17 → 'Strong'. Summing all ten subscales gives the 30–180 total.

## The report

The report first brings the reader to mind of a specific person who hurt them. It then names their **strongest motivations**, each described warmly with a Scripture, shows **all ten ranked**, gives a gentle overall read, and — because this is sensitive — it is **delivered as a private link only, never emailed as a full report**. Credit to the Enright Forgiveness Motivation Inventory and the underlying research is shown on the report.

Every report carries the Mission USA credit line: _"Assessment by Mission USA of the Congregational Holiness Church. Get yours at assessments.chchurch.com."_ Reports can be printed or saved as PDF, and are saved to the taker's profile.
